Psychological safety was first identified and named by Harvard Business School professor Amy Edmondson in her 1999 paper "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams".



It's important because it allows individuals to feel comfortable sharing their thoughts, ideas, and concerns without fear of negative consequences. When teams feel safe, individuals are more likely to speak up, express dissenting opinions, and take risks. This leads to improved decision-making, increased creativity and innovation, and better team performance. Furthermore, psychological safety is also linked to better employee well-being and job satisfaction.

Not addressing psychological safety can have a number of negative impacts on an organization and its employees.

  • It can lead to a lack of trust and communication within teams, which can hinder the flow of information and lead to poor decision-making.
  • Employees may be less likely to speak up and share their ideas, which can stifle innovation and creativity.
  • Without psychological safety, employees may be more likely to experience stress, burnout, and other negative mental health outcomes.
  • When employees don't feel safe to speak up, it can lead to a lack of accountability and an increased risk of errors and safety incidents.
  • When employees feel unsafe, they may be less engaged, less productive, and more likely to leave the organization.
  • It can also create an environment in which discrimination and harassment can thrive, which can lead to serious legal and reputation risks for the organization.
  • Overall, not addressing psychological safety can have significant negative impacts on both employees and the organization as a whole.
