The OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework was developed by Andy Grove, the former CEO and chairman of Intel Corporation. Grove first introduced the framework at Intel in the 1970s as a way to align the efforts of the company's employees with the overall business strategy and to track progress towards achieving strategic goals. The framework was later popularized in Grove's 1999 book "High Output Management". Since then, OKRs have been adopted by many other organizations and companies, both big and small, across a wide range of industries, as a way to set and track measurable goals and outcomes.



The framework consists of a specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound objective, and a set of key results that measure progress towards that objective. OKRs are used by organizations and teams to align their efforts and track progress towards achieving their objectives. OKRs are commonly used in businesses and startups, but can also be used in other organizations such as non-profits and government agencies.

Objective: Increase website traffic by 50% in Q2

This objective is specific (increase website traffic), measurable (by 50%), achievable (with the right strategies and tactics), relevant (to the overall business goals), and time-bound (in Q2). The objective is clear and easy to understand, and the team can work together to develop key results (KRs) that will help them achieve this objective. For example, some key results (KRs) that could support this objective could be:
  • KR1: Increase organic search traffic by 30% through on-page SEO optimization
  • KR2: Increase referral traffic by 20% through targeted outreach and partnership building
  • KR3: Increase social media traffic by 15% through a targeted social media marketing campaign
These key results will help the team understand how they will achieve the objective of increasing website traffic by 50% in Q2, and they can track their progress on these key results to ensure they are on track to achieve the overall objective.

A Key Result (KR) is a measurable outcome that helps track progress towards achieving an objective. Here's an example of a Key Result (KR) that supports the objective of "Increase website traffic by 50% in Q2" that I provided earlier:

  • Key Result: Increase organic search traffic by 30% through on-page SEO optimization
This key result is specific (increase organic search traffic by 30%), measurable (through website analytics), and time-bound (in Q2). It supports the overall objective of increasing website traffic by 50% in Q2, and it is clear how it will contribute to achieving that objective. To achieve this key result, the team might implement a number of tactics such as:
  • Conducting keyword research to identify high-value search terms
  • Optimizing website content for those keywords
  • Improving website architecture to make it more search engine friendly
  • Building backlinks from high-quality websites
  • The team can track progress towards this key result by monitoring website analytics to see if organic search traffic is increasing, and by using specific metrics such as search engine rankings, click-through rates, and conversion rates.
Also, this KR is a leading indicator, as it is something that directly impacts the objective, and it is something the team can act upon in order to achieve the final outcome.
